Signal — Issue #1 · May 3, 2026

Most AI features feel bolted on. Here's what ships when they don't.

You've used them. The chatbot in the corner that summarizes a doc you already read. The "AI insights" panel that's slower than the dashboard it replaced. The autocomplete that's right 60% of the time and wrong in ways you can't predict. AI as a marketing asset, not a feature.

The frustrating part: these aren't bad models. The models are remarkable. The problem is that the feature is not the model. The feature is the model plus everything around it — and most of "around it" is missing.

What's missing in a bolted-on AI feature

  1. The eval harness. You cannot ship a probability distribution as if it were a button. Teams shipping AI features that don't break maintain ~50–100 golden examples and run them against every model update, prompt change, and pipeline change. Without that, you're shipping vibes.
  2. The latency budget. Users tolerate 1–2 seconds. Past 10 seconds, they leave. If your feature can't hit the budget, you need streaming, progress indicators, or a different shape — not "let me just throw another tool call in there."
  3. The cost cap. Track AI cost per user, per feature, per model. If your cost-per-user crosses your margin threshold and you're not alerting on it, you're bleeding money on the feature that was supposed to make you more.
  4. The failure mode. What does the user see when the model returns garbage, the API times out, or the request gets flagged? "Sorry, something went wrong" is not a failure mode. A graceful fallback that still gets the user to their next step is.
  5. The placement. AI features in the corner of the app are AI features the user doesn't use. The good ones show up at the moment of decision, make the next step obvious, and get out of the way.

That's it. That's the list. Five things. None of them are the model.

What's actually shipping in 2026

A few signals worth tracking:

  • Anthropic shipped Managed Agents — a hosted runtime for Claude + MCP that handles secure sandboxing, long-running sessions, scoped permissions, tool execution, and tracing. The infrastructure problem most teams solved themselves badly is now somebody else's problem.
  • Replit went from $2M to $144M ARR in a year by moving to usage-based pricing on its AI coding bot. The lesson isn't "switch to usage-based" — it's that AI features can have non-linear monetization if the feature is good enough that users want more of it.
  • Inventive AI is a clean example of focused vertical AI: RFP and security questionnaire automation for SaaS revenue teams, claiming 10× faster response with 95%+ accuracy. One narrow workflow, instrumented end-to-end. That's the shape that wins.
  • The cautionary signal: "compound systems" and "agentic workflows" are getting buzzy enough that most teams will lose six months building generic agent infrastructure they don't need. The actual advice from teams who've shipped: don't build a custom agent framework, vector DB, or eval pipeline before you have 100 paying users. Direct API calls, pgvector, manual evaluation. Add complexity only when you hit real scaling problems.

What I'd ship in a B2B SaaS app this week

Two concrete features I'd ship right now if I were your CTO:

1. The "explain this number" button on every dashboard metric. Not a chatbot. A contextual button that, when clicked, runs a single agent with access to your warehouse, returns a 3-sentence explanation of why this number is what it is right now, with one suggested action. Latency under 2s. Eval harness against your top 20 metrics. Cost-capped at $0.10/click. Cost falls when most clicks are repeat questions and the answer is cached.

2. The deal-shaping co-pilot in your CRM. When a sales rep opens a deal, a sidebar surfaces three things: the most analogous closed-won deals from the last quarter, the specific stalls historically associated with this stage and segment, and a recommended next action. Not generative copy. Retrieval-first. The model's job is summarization and ranking, not invention.

Both of these are 2–3 weeks of work, in your stack, with the boring parts done.
