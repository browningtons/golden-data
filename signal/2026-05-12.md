The dbt team published their 2026 Text-to-SQL benchmark this week. It should be the only thing AI/BI buyers read in May.
</p>

Same questions, two paths to SQL. With the model alone — Sonnet 4.6, GPT-5.3 Codex — accuracy clocks 64.5%. Identical numbers. Two model families, same ceiling. Pipe the same questions through the dbt Semantic Layer and both jump to roughly 100% on covered queries.

The model isn't the variable. The data model is.

Everyone in this industry has spent six months arguing about which foundation lab pulled ahead this quarter, while the actual lever sat in the metric layer the whole time. Microsoft's quiet retirement of Power BI Q&A — announced for December, formally replaced by Copilot — is the same admission. Natural language on bare schemas doesn't work at production accuracy. Natural language on a curated semantic layer does.

If your AI feature is sitting on raw tables, no amount of model swapping fixes it. The work is upstream.

## What I'd ship in your app this week

Two features I'd build right now if I ran product at a sales ops platform or an ad platform's internal sales-tooling team.

**1. The Tuesday forecast brief.** The user moment: Monday, 5pm, a RevOps lead staring at tomorrow's 9am forecast call. Today they spend 45 minutes pulling reports and reading Slack. Tomorrow, a brief lands in their inbox at 7am. The shape: an agent grounded in the CRM and a semantic layer, with three tools — pipeline delta query, opportunity history lookup, rep activity rollup. Latency budget: 90 seconds, run overnight. Cost ceiling: $0.50 per brief. Eval shape: 30 golden examples of known deal patterns — slip, pull-in, churn risk, new logo — paired with the commentary a lead would actually act on. Success at week two: the lead walks into the call already knowing the three deals worth talking about. The slide-flipping ritual is gone.

**2. The RFP triage memo.** For ad-platform sales orgs — the Reddits, Snaps, Pinterests. The user moment: an advertiser fills out an RFP. An AE has 30 minutes before the pitch call and is otherwise searching the CRM by hand. The shape: retrieval-first, single model call. The agent pulls 3-5 analogous past campaigns matched on vertical, budget, and geo, with their performance vs. forecast and the segments that actually converted. The model summarizes and ranks. It does not invent. Latency: 5-8 seconds. Cost ceiling: $0.25 per memo. Eval: 50 closed-won and closed-lost analogues, scored on whether the matches and segments would have helped the rep prep. Success at week two: prep time drops from 30 minutes to 5, and triaged briefs start to outclose untriaged ones.

Both work for the same reason. They're cheap, narrow, and sit inside a workflow the rep already runs. The model is the smallest part of either build. The semantic layer underneath — clean opportunity history, defined metric definitions, a real concept of "analogous campaign" — is the part that takes the time.

That's the work. That's also the moat.
