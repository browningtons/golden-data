HubSpot and The Trade Desk both reported Q1 last week. Both have flagship AI bets. One earned a raised full-year guide. One earned an analyst downgrade. The variable wasn't the model. It was who controlled the upgrade.

Breeze runs on credits the customer opts into. Total credits consumed grew 67% quarter over quarter. Active core-seat users grew 90% year over year. HubSpot lifted FY26 guidance to $3.7B and hit its 2027 margin target a year early.

Kokai is the opposite. It's a forced platform migration with pricing customers are still pushing back on, on top of a partial UI rollback in September. Q1 came in at $689M, +12% — fine on its face. The Q2 guide missed, William Blair cut to Market Perform on May 8, the stock printed $21.52 on May 11. Down 43% year to date.

Two AI bets. One earnings week. Same scoreboard. The pricing model and adoption shape are part of the AI feature now, not separate from it. The vendor who forces the upgrade is selling last year's product at a higher price.

## What's actually moving this week

**1. HubSpot's AI is a credit business, and the credits compound.** Q1 revenue +23% YoY on May 6, ~300K customers, FY26 guidance raised. Breeze credits consumed grew 67% QoQ. Core-seat active users grew 90% YoY. The renewal isn't where the AI revenue prints. The mid-quarter credit-pack upgrade is. If your AI feature isn't metered in something the customer can choose to consume more of, your AI ARR is renewal-gated by definition.

**2. The Trade Desk got downgraded on the back of Kokai.** $689M Q1 revenue (+12%), Q2 guidance missed, William Blair downgraded to Market Perform on May 8. Kokai has been forced on customers since mid-2025, with a periodic-table UI rollback in September after public resistance. Stock $21.52 on May 11, down 43% YTD. Customer-led adoption is a moat. Forced adoption is a churn vector. The lesson generalizes — the AI rebuild that auto-migrates the customer is a renewal-risk event disguised as a roadmap milestone.

**3. Snowflake's World Tour landed dbt Fusion as the headline on May 12.** Snowflake and dbt Labs commercialized native dbt Projects inside Snowflake — the orchestration layer is now compute-adjacent. If you sell a separate orchestration or transformation product into Snowflake accounts, the bundling slide hit your CEO's desk this week. The semantic-layer consolidation Tableau and Fabric kicked off last week is now downstream of the transformation-layer consolidation. Two layers gone in two weeks.

**4. A new 5-model hallucination benchmark dropped this month.** DigitalApplied ran 5,000 prompts across factual recall, citation accuracy, and code reference. Frontier hallucination rates sit between 3.1% and 19.1%. Citation accuracy is the worst family at 12.4% even with extended thinking on. Retrieval grounding cut rates 75–90%. Tool grounding cut them 65–80%. The architecture is the safety story. If your eval is sized for the model and not for the retrieval, you're measuring the wrong half.

## What I'd ship in your app this week

**Feature one: the AI-credit receipt, on the admin's billing screen.** An SMB admin opens billing at week's end. Today they see "$X consumed." Tomorrow they see what the agents actually did — 47 prospecting research runs that produced 12 sourced leads, 8 customer-message drafts the rep sent verbatim, 23 deal-loss reviews that flagged 4 at-risk accounts. Every line cites the row in the activity log. The credit-pack upgrade decision stops being faith-based.

<ul class="list-bullet">
  <li><strong>Shape.</strong> Aggregation over the agent-invocation log, plus one grounded LLM call per agent type to render the human-readable line. Cache aggressively.</li>
  <li><strong>Latency budget.</strong> 5 seconds, lazy on screen open. Weekly view, not real-time.</li>
  <li><strong>Cost ceiling.</strong> $0.05 per render. One render per admin per day, hard-capped. The receipt cannot cost more than the upsell it enables.</li>
  <li><strong>Eval.</strong> 150 golden receipts written by CS managers, scored on "would this admin renew or expand the credit pack after reading it." Re-run weekly.</li>
  <li><strong>Two weeks in.</strong> A 15% lift in monthly credit-pack upgrades on the cohort that saw the receipt, vs. the cohort that didn't. If you don't see it, the line items aren't tied to outcomes the admin already recognizes as value.</li>
</ul>

**Feature two: the RFP-draft assistant, on the sales rep's inbox.** An advertiser brief lands at 9am. Within 90 seconds, an agent assembles a draft response — matched inventory windows, comparable campaign performance from the last 90 days in that vertical, three pricing options, the standard legal boilerplate. The rep edits and sends by lunch instead of by Thursday.

<ul class="list-bullet">
  <li><strong>Shape.</strong> Async agent on the inbound-mail webhook. Retrieval over the RFP library, the live inventory calendar, and the last 12 months of campaigns in the advertiser's vertical.</li>
  <li><strong>Latency budget.</strong> 90 seconds end to end. The constraint is inventory freshness, not paint time.</li>
  <li><strong>Cost ceiling.</strong> $0.60 per draft. One draft per inbound RFP. Wired to the rep-hour it replaces.</li>
  <li><strong>Eval.</strong> 100 senior-AE-reviewed drafts, scored on "would I send this with fewer than 30% edits." Re-run on every prompt or retrieval change.</li>
  <li><strong>Two weeks in.</strong> Median rep response time on inbound RFPs drops from three days to under 24 hours on the cohort with the feature. If it doesn't drop, the retrieval is wrong — the rep is rewriting from scratch and the draft was theater.</li>
</ul>

Both ship in two to three weeks with the team you already have. Both have kill switches wired to the cost-per-call ceiling. Neither requires a model contract you don't already have.
