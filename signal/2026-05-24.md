Microsoft did the opposite of what Salesforce just did. On May 1 they shipped Microsoft 365 E7 — the first new enterprise tier since E5 in 2015 — at $99 per user per month, with Agent 365 bundled inside. Three days from now Salesforce reports Agentforce as its own segment for the first time. One vendor buried AI in a higher-priced tier. The other one is making it a line on the income statement. Same month.

The split matters for any B2B SaaS founder pricing an AI feature right now. Bundle the AI in and the buyer doesn't see the cost — but the renewal champion can't defend the line either, and your CFO can't tell investors what the AI is earning. Break it out and the AI feature has to carry its own number every quarter — auditable, comparable, expensable. Two architectures. Your procurement team is about to ask which one you're running.

## What's actually moving this week

**1. Microsoft 365 E7 went GA on May 1 at $99 per user per month.** First new enterprise SKU since E5 in 2015. E5 plus Copilot plus Entra Suite plus Agent 365, bundled. Field teams are pushing every E5 renewal toward it. Watch the Copilot Credit consumption line — agentic workloads layer variable cost on top of the seat price, and CIOs deploying agentic workflows are reporting effective per-user costs north of $200. The bet is that the bundle hides the AI cost long enough to make it sticky.

**2. TikTok shipped an MCP server for its Ads platform.** At TikTok World last week. Third social platform this quarter to make its ad ecosystem agent-callable, after Reddit Max went GA and Snowflake/Databricks shipped MCP on the data side. The implication for ad-tech builders: your customers' agents will query the platform's inventory directly. Integration stops being your differentiator and starts being a contract you publish.

**3. The Open Semantic Interchange v0.1 spec was finalized.** Snowflake, Salesforce/Tableau, dbt Labs, and BlackRock co-authored. Apache 2 license, public repo. The metric-drift-across-tools problem just got a standard. For BI builders the question is no longer "do we ship a semantic layer." It's "do we publish OSI on the wire by year-end."

**4. Tech layoffs hit a 2023 high while AI capex crossed $725B.** CBS pegged AI as 26% of April's tech job cuts; Challenger had the same number. Block cut 40% of staff. Oracle eliminated ~30,000 roles. Meta's 8,000 cuts take effect May 20. Read it as the AI feature in your roadmap competing with payroll for the same dollar at every B2B SaaS company. The CFO already internalized that math. Procurement has too.

## What I'd ship in your app this week

**Feature one: the AI consumption meter, on the customer success record.** A CSM opens an enterprise account on Monday morning. Pinned at the top: "AI usage this period: $4,200 of $5,000 contracted. Up 18% MoM. Top driver: agent runs in the campaign builder, 62% of total. Three other accounts in your book are tracking the same trajectory." The meter is the early warning. It either flags renewal upside — the customer is hitting the ceiling and needs an upsell conversation — or renewal risk, the customer is paying for AI they're not using.

<ul class="list-bullet">
  <li><strong>Shape.</strong> Daily aggregation job on the inference logs. Single grounded LLM call to render the human-readable summary. Deterministic ranking against the CSM's other accounts.</li>
  <li><strong>Latency budget.</strong> 3 seconds, lazy-render when the panel opens. Pre-computed nightly.</li>
  <li><strong>Cost ceiling.</strong> $0.05 per panel render. Hard daily cap per CSM seat.</li>
  <li><strong>Eval.</strong> 150 golden panels reviewed by senior CSMs, scored on "would this have changed how I prepped for the QBR." Re-run weekly.</li>
  <li><strong>Two weeks in.</strong> 30% of QBRs reference the consumption meter as a discussion starter. If you don't see it, the panel isn't pinned high enough on the account record.</li>
</ul>

**Feature two: the metric-definition explainer, on the BI dashboard.** A user opens a dashboard, a metric jumped 14% week-over-week, they hover. A small panel renders: this metric's definition (citing the OSI-style semantic model), the data sources it pulls from, who last changed the definition and when, and the three most likely business reasons for the move. Each cited back to the row of data it was drawn from. The dashboard stops being the answer. The metric becomes the answer.

<ul class="list-bullet">
  <li><strong>Shape.</strong> Retrieval over the semantic model, the change log, and the last 30 days of underlying data. Single grounded LLM call to render the explanation.</li>
  <li><strong>Latency budget.</strong> 4 seconds, hover-to-paint.</li>
  <li><strong>Cost ceiling.</strong> $0.03 per call. Hard cap. Cache aggressively per metric per day.</li>
  <li><strong>Eval.</strong> 200 golden hover-explanations written by senior analytics engineers, scored on "would this have stopped the analyst from re-deriving the metric." Re-run weekly.</li>
  <li><strong>Two weeks in.</strong> 25% drop in "what does this metric mean" Slack threads in the analytics channel, measured on Slack export.</li>
</ul>

Both features ship in two to three weeks with the team you already have. Both have a kill switch wired to the cost-per-call ceiling. Neither needs a model contract you don't have.
